# Grant Proposal

## What this skill does

Produces key grant proposal sections that meet funder expectations for rigor, specificity, and impact orientation. Whether you are writing a letter of inquiry, a full federal application, or a private foundation proposal, this skill generates compelling narrative that connects community need to programmatic response to measurable outcomes.

1. **Needs statement** — data-backed articulation of the problem your program addresses, grounded in local, regional, or national evidence
2. **Program description and goals** — clear description of what you will do, who you will serve, and how activities connect to outcomes
3. **Measurable objectives** — SMART objectives formatted to satisfy outcome-based funders and federal reporting requirements
4. **Evaluation methodology** — data collection plan, success metrics, and reporting timeline
5. **Budget narrative** — line-item justification that explains the "why" behind each cost and demonstrates stewardship

## How to invoke

```
/grant-proposal
```

Claude will ask:
- What is the grant opportunity? (funder name, program name, deadline, and award amount range)
- What organization is applying, and what is the program or project being funded?
- Who are the target beneficiaries, and how many will be served?
- What data or evidence demonstrates the need in your community?
- What is the proposed budget total, and what are the major cost categories?

## Workflow steps

### Step 1 — Funder and Program Alignment
Claude reviews the funder's stated priorities and aligns the proposal framing to match their language, focus areas, and theory of change. Proposals that mirror funder vocabulary score higher in competitive review.

### Step 2 — Needs Statement Drafting
Claude builds the needs statement using a three-layer structure: national/state context → local community data → gap that your program fills. Data citations are flagged for you to verify and source.

### Step 3 — Program Description and SMART Objectives
Claude writes the program description in activity-to-outcome order, then generates 3–5 SMART objectives (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) that align with those activities.

### Step 4 — Evaluation Plan and Budget Narrative
Claude produces a concise evaluation methodology with data sources, collection timeline, and reporting plan, followed by a line-by-line budget narrative that justifies each expense to the funder's standard.

## Example outputs

### Example: Environmental Education Grant for a Land Trust

**Input:** Applying to the California Environmental Education Foundation for a $75,000 grant. Organization: Sierraview Land Trust. Program: "Roots and Rivers" — a field-based environmental education program for Title I elementary students in Kern County. Serves 500 students/year across 8 schools. Need: Only 22% of Kern County Title I students have access to outdoor environmental education. Budget: $75,000 (staff, transportation, curriculum, supplies).

**Grant proposal sections draft:**
```
GRANT PROPOSAL — ROOTS AND RIVERS ENVIRONMENTAL EDUCATION PROGRAM
Applicant:   Sierraview Land Trust
Funder:      California Environmental Education Foundation
Program:     Environmental Literacy for Underserved Youth
Award Range: $50,000–$100,000
Project Year: July 1, 2026 – June 30, 2027

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
NEEDS STATEMENT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
California's Central Valley is home to some of the state's most
biodiverse ecosystems — and some of its most environmentally burdened
communities. Kern County ranks in the 90th percentile or above for
pesticide exposure, PM2.5 particulate matter, and groundwater
contamination threats under CalEnviroScreen 4.0. Despite these urgent
environmental conditions, fewer than one in four Title I elementary
students in Kern County has access to outdoor environmental education
during the school year (California Department of Education, 2025 LCAP
survey data). Statewide, students in low-income communities are half
as likely as their peers in higher-income districts to participate in
field-based nature education (Lawrence Hall of Science, 2024).

This gap matters beyond environmental literacy. Research consistently
demonstrates that place-based outdoor education improves academic
engagement, science test scores, and social-emotional development
among elementary-age students — outcomes that Title I schools are
explicitly prioritizing. A 2023 meta-analysis published in the Journal
of Environmental Education found that students participating in at
least 10 hours of field-based environmental education per year showed
a 14% improvement in science assessment scores compared to control
groups.

Sierraview Land Trust stewards over 28,000 acres of oak woodland,
riparian corridor, and native grassland in Kern and Tulare Counties —
extraordinary natural classrooms that are, paradoxically, inaccessible
to most children who live within miles of them. Transportation costs,
time constraints, and curriculum alignment barriers have historically
prevented Title I schools from accessing these sites. Roots and Rivers
was designed specifically to remove those barriers.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
PROGRAM DESCRIPTION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Roots and Rivers provides 500 Title I elementary students (grades 3–5)
at eight Kern County schools with a three-part environmental education
experience: (1) two in-classroom preparation sessions led by trained
Land Trust educators; (2) a full-day guided field experience on Sierraview
preserved land; and (3) a post-visit classroom lesson that connects field
observations to California Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS).

Each cohort of students participates in one complete sequence per year.
Field experiences rotate across three Land Trust sites — Poso Creek
Riparian Preserve, Tejon Oak Woodland, and Sand Ridge Natural Area — so
that students encounter distinct habitat types and understand how place
shapes ecological community. All curriculum materials are available in
English and Spanish. Each in-classroom session is co-facilitated with
the classroom teacher to build teacher capacity for ongoing environmental
integration.

Roots and Rivers is currently in its third year of operation. In Year 2
(2024–2025), the program served 412 students across six schools, with
97% of participating teachers reporting that the program strengthened
their students' connection to local ecosystems and 89% reporting
measurable increases in student engagement in science content.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
GOALS AND MEASURABLE OBJECTIVES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
GOAL 1: Expand access to field-based environmental education for
Title I students in Kern County.

  Objective 1.1: By June 30, 2027, Roots and Rivers will provide
  complete three-part environmental education sequences to a minimum
  of 500 students across eight Title I elementary schools in Kern County,
  representing a 21% increase over Year 2 (412 students).

  Objective 1.2: By June 30, 2027, at least 85% of participating
  students will demonstrate measurable gains in environmental science
  knowledge, as measured by a validated pre/post assessment aligned
  to NGSS Earth Systems standards.

GOAL 2: Build teacher capacity for ongoing environmental education
integration.

  Objective 2.1: By June 30, 2027, at least 90% of participating
  classroom teachers will report increased confidence in facilitating
  nature-based learning, as measured by a post-program teacher survey.

  Objective 2.2: By December 31, 2026, Sierraview will conduct two
  professional development workshops for participating teachers,
  providing a minimum of 6 curriculum integration resources per teacher.

GOAL 3: Deepen student connection to local natural heritage.

  Objective 3.1: By June 30, 2027, at least 75% of participating
  students will report, on a post-visit student survey, that they
  feel a personal connection to the natural areas in their region
  — a 10-point increase over Year 2 baseline (65%).

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
EVALUATION METHODOLOGY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Sierraview will use a mixed-methods evaluation approach combining
quantitative pre/post assessments with qualitative feedback from
teachers and students.

DATA COLLECTION:
  • Student pre/post knowledge assessment (validated 15-item instrument;
    administered by classroom teacher before first in-classroom session
    and after post-visit lesson)
  • Student connection survey (8-item Likert scale; administered
    post-field experience)
  • Teacher satisfaction and capacity survey (10-item; administered
    within 2 weeks of program completion)
  • Program participation log (student count, school, grade, site visited)

TIMELINE:
  July–August 2026    Staff and teacher orientation; pre-assessment tools finalized
  September 2026      School-year cohorts begin; pre-assessments administered
  October–April 2027  Field experiences and post-visit lessons delivered
  May 2027            All post-assessments and surveys administered
  June 2027           Data analysis complete; final report to funder

REPORTING: Sierraview will provide a mid-year progress report in
January 2027 and a final evaluation report by July 31, 2027, including
all quantitative outcome data and narrative summary of program learnings.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
BUDGET NARRATIVE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
TOTAL REQUEST: $75,000

Personnel — $48,500 (65% of budget)
  Environmental Education Coordinator (0.60 FTE): $38,000
  The Coordinator manages all school relationships, delivers in-classroom
  sessions, leads field experiences, and administers evaluation tools.
  At 0.60 FTE of a $63,333 annual salary, this reflects the portion of
  the Coordinator's time attributable to Roots and Rivers vs. other
  programs. The Roots and Rivers program represents the Coordinator's
  primary assignment for 9 months of the year.

  Program Assistant (0.25 FTE, seasonal): $10,500
  The Assistant supports field day logistics, transportation coordination,
  and data entry for 500 students across 8 schools and 3 sites. This
  role is critical on field days, where 60-student cohorts require
  two-staff facilitation for safety and quality.

Transportation — $14,000 (19% of budget)
  Transportation is the most significant non-personnel barrier to
  participation for Title I schools. Sierraview contracts with a
  certified school transportation provider for all field day transport.
  At an average of $700 per round trip (school to preserve and return),
  serving 20 school field days (8 schools × 2–3 cohort visits each),
  transportation costs total $14,000. No cost to schools.

Curriculum and Supplies — $6,500 (9% of budget)
  Printing of bilingual curriculum packets (500 students × $6/packet
  = $3,000); field science supplies including hand lenses, water quality
  test kits, and plant identification guides replenished annually ($2,000);
  and educator training materials for professional development workshops
  ($1,500).

Indirect Costs — $6,000 (8% of budget)
  Indirect costs are charged at Sierraview's federally negotiated indirect
  cost rate of 8%, applied to direct program costs. These costs cover
  organizational infrastructure — accounting, HR, and facilities —
  that support program delivery.
```

**Letter of inquiry variant (250 words):**
```
Sierraview Land Trust respectfully requests $75,000 from the California
Environmental Education Foundation to expand Roots and Rivers, our
field-based environmental education program serving Title I students
in Kern County.

Kern County's Title I elementary students live surrounded by remarkable
natural landscapes — and largely cut off from them. Fewer than 22% have
access to outdoor environmental education during the school year, despite
compelling evidence that field-based science learning improves academic
engagement and environmental stewardship outcomes. Roots and Rivers
removes the transportation, curriculum alignment, and cost barriers that
keep schools from accessing Sierraview's preserved lands.

In Year 3 (2026–2027), Roots and Rivers will serve 500 students across
eight schools with a three-part sequence: in-classroom preparation,
a guided full-day field experience, and a standards-aligned post-visit
lesson. We will measure outcomes through validated pre/post assessments
and teacher surveys, targeting 85% of students demonstrating measurable
knowledge gains.

Sierraview brings 25 years of land stewardship and seven years of
education programming to this work. A CEEF grant would enable us to
reach 88 more students than in Year 2, deepen teacher capacity, and
generate evaluation data that supports the case for sustained school
partnerships.

We welcome the opportunity to submit a full proposal. Thank you for
your consideration.
```
